## Chapter 1: The Ordinary Morning

### Section 1.1: Awakening

The alarm clock shrieked at 6:47 AM—thirteen minutes before it was supposed to, which Denis found annoying but not unusual. His apartment, a modest one-bedroom in the heart of the city, was bathed in the grey light of early morning. He lay in bed for a moment in complete silence. Not a usual quiet and relaxing silence, but an unusual, eerie ringing silence—a disturbing absence of anything, anything familiar. He tried to speak, but heard nothing. Nothing at all. He quickly strode to the window, swung it open and, finally, he heard the street. Loud, unpleasant, dirty, but familiar. Car horns blared, voices shouted from the sidewalk below, the constant hum of urban life reasserted itself like a lifeline thrown to a drowning man. He tried to speak to himself, and he heard it—the dry voice of his own, hoarse and uncertain, but unmistakably real.

He stood for a moment near the window, panic fading away, but not the emptiness. A strange feeling settled in his chest—a feeling of change, an anticipation of something new and unfamiliar. He tried to understand it, to catch that moment of clarity, but it slipped away like water through his fingers, leaving only the residue of dread. He stood there in his underwear, messy hair hanging over his brow, his swollen, aging face—the face of a man of thirty-three years old.

The clock rang again at 7 AM, returning Denis to reality. But the sound was different from the first time. It wasn't a ring at all—it was the usual random song from an internet radio, some forgettable pop melody bleeding out of his alarm speaker. Maybe he had dreamed the first time. Just a bad dream, the kind that leaves residue on your mind for hours. Too many movies before bed, maybe. Or too much stress. Or something else he couldn't quite name.

Denis shook his head and began his morning routine: shower, coffee, toast. Everything methodical. Everything predictable. He dressed in his usual attire—grey slacks, a white button-up shirt, a charcoal blazer. The uniform of an ordinary man living an ordinary life.

In a few moments, he was in his car, warming it up for a moment, looking in the mirrors, checking his surroundings—without memorizing or even understanding what he was looking at. Just a regular routine, things he had done for many years. His body was working on its own while its owner was deep in his thoughts, lost somewhere else entirely. Maybe there was nothing special around him. He heard the familiar sounds of the street—people talking, car horns honking, some road works nearby. But, in fact, there were no people in sight. No cars running on the street. No one around him for a few blocks. The sounds continued—voices, horns, the mechanical whine of construction equipment—but there was nothing there. Just lifeless pavement lined up with cars lined, black cars, all of them were black, stretching in all directions.

Perhaps it was too early. Perhaps people hadn't started their commute yet. But routine is routine. It should continue no matter what. No attention, no thoughts on the matter should enter his mind. Just work. What was that? Another business trip. A client once more with damaged equipment. Once more he would have to inspect, evaluate, and give a hefty price. Learning from one's own mistakes can't be cheap, after all.

The road through the city and out was unremarkable. People were on the streets—normal people going about their normal business. Usual traffic. Maybe a few close calls with rather nervous drivers, but nothing out of the ordinary for a Tuesday morning. The phantom emptiness Denis had sensed earlier seemed to have lifted, replaced by the reassuring chaos of urban life. He made several work-related calls on the way. By 9 AM, he was finally out of the city, the buildings giving way to suburbs and then open road.

The road was smooth beneath his tires, and the engine purred softly. The tank was full, and a long road stretched ahead—maybe a few stops along the way.
